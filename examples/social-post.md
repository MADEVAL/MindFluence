# Example: Social Media Post (LinkedIn)

**Context:** Promoting a time-tracking SaaS to freelance designers.
**Request:** "Write a LinkedIn post about why freelancers undercharge."

---

[TONE: warm-human × expert-calm]
[BIASES ENGAGED: Loss Aversion, Availability Heuristic, Anchoring, Confirmation Bias, Social Proof]
[TARGET ACTION: Save + Comment + Click link for free tool]

---

I lost $23,000 before I understood this.

For 3 years, I charged clients based on what "felt right" for a project. Logo? $500. Full brand? $2,000. I'd look at the brief, guess the hours, add a buffer, and send the proposal.

I was wrong. Consistently. By a lot.

Here's what I discovered when I actually tracked my time for 30 days:

A "simple" website redesign I charged $1,800 for? Took 41 hours. That's $43.90/hour — before taxes, before software, before the unpaid admin time the client never sees.

The revision requests I never billed for? 11 hours in one month. The "quick calls" scattered across the week? Another 6 hours. The project management, the invoicing, the follow-ups? I wasn't billing for any of it.

My real hourly rate wasn't $75. It was $31.

That's the hidden tax of not tracking your time: you don't know what you're actually earning. And what you can't measure, you can't fix.

After 30 days of tracking, I raised my rates 40%. I lost one client. The rest stayed. Because the clients who value your work don't leave when you charge what you're worth — they leave when you deliver less than you promised.

I built a dead-simple time tracker to make this 30-day audit painless. No bloated features. Start the timer. Stop the timer. See the math.

It's free. Link in the first comment.

What's the one task you suspect is eating your profits but you've never measured it?

---

[RATIONALE]
- **Loss Aversion:** The opening — "$23,000 lost" — immediately activates the brain's threat-detection. Readers who are freelancers see a concrete loss figure and project it onto their own situation.
- **Availability Heuristic:** The detailed personal story (41 hours, $43.90/hr, 11 hours of revisions, 6 hours of calls) creates vivid, recallable images. Abstract advice ("track your time") is forgettable. The story embeds.
- **Anchoring:** The $1,800 vs $43.90/hr contrast creates cognitive dissonance. The "real rate" of $31 becomes the pain anchor against which the solution feels like relief.
- **Confirmation Bias:** "The clients who value your work don't leave when you charge what you're worth." This tells the reader they were RIGHT to suspect they're undercharging. Dopamine hit = engagement.
- **Social Proof:** The CTA asks for a specific comment. Comments = social proof for the post. The more comments, the more the LinkedIn algorithm amplifies, and the more new readers see it as valuable (Bandwagon).
- **Sunk Cost (in CTA):** The link is positioned as the natural next step after consuming a detailed, valuable post. The reader has already invested 2+ minutes — the "free tool" feels like an earned reward, not a pitch.
